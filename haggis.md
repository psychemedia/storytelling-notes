# The Wild Haggis

The wild haggis is a rare creature, the food of the poor, traditionally, until it came to be hunted by the gentry, and was driven all but extinct. There are still a few wild haggis to be found in the highlands of Scotland, but the locations are protected and known only to a few.

The haggis that do remain come in two forms, *haggis scoticus dextra* and *haggis scoticus sinistra*. As you will know, *dextra* and *sinistra* relate to chirality, or handedness, *dextra* for right-handed, *sinistra* for left-handed.

But in the case of the haggis, this relates to which of their legs are the longer. For wild haggis live on or above the tree line in the higher parts of the highlands and have a fascinating lifecycle.

After fledging, the young start their lives on the lower parts of the hillside. Depending on which species of haggis we are talking about, the legs on either their left hand side or their right hand side are longer than on the other side.

This adaptation allows them to hunt — or graze — scientists still argue to this day as to whether the original wild haggis were herbivores or carnivorous predators — by circling their way around the mountainside.

For the *haggis scoticus dextra*, the longer right legs, they would make their way anti-clockwise around the hillside. For *haggis scoticus sinistra*, they would make their way round clockwise. [A small experiment demonstrates this. If you model the short leg by putting that foot flat on the floor, and make the other leg longer by pointing your toes, you'll see that you start to pivot round the "shorter" leg. So your right leg is longer, as with the *haggis scoticus __dextra__*, you pivot on the left foot, and then to go in an *anti-clockwise* drection, and when your left leg is longer (*haggis scoticus __sinistra__*), you pivot around your *right* foot and go round clockwise. Another way to think of it is that as you go up the mountain, you need your shorter leg to be on the inside. So for a short left leg, which is to say,  *haggis scoticus __dextra__* with the longer right leg, the center of the clock is on the left and you will go round an up in an anti-clockwise direction.] Obviously you can't go downhill, because you'd just go into a roll. It's also worth noting that the two species can't interbreed becuase there's no way for them to line uo the necessary body parts. So they represent a remarkable example of parallel evolution since the two branches separated many hundreds of thousands of years ago, especially when you consider that each species competes for dominance of each mountain in each and every single generation.

The haggis have their young near the top of the hill, where the path is still quite wide and then proceed to a nest right on the top of the hill in a small hollow, scraped into the hill top. The young are born behind the female, who then proceeds around the top of the hill, until they come to the next haggis young ahead at which point they push the young, curled up in the shape of a ball, until it catches up with its mother.
