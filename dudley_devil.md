# Theophilus Dunn, the Dudley Devil

I first came across *Theophilus Dunn*, also know as the Dudley Devil, a fortune teller from the Black Country way, from the story behind a song — *"The Tipton Slasher"* — penned by Jess and Al, aka *Berlingo Flick*.

```{admonition} NOT the Dudley Devil, January 18143
:class: dropdown
In *Hereford Journal*, [Wednesday 11 January 1843](https://britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000398/18430111/008/0002).

Credulity.—At a weekly meeting of the Board of Guardians of the Stourbridge Poor-law Union, held on Friday, Mr. Chadwick, the Governor, reported that an aged female pauper, who had been permitted to leave the house on Monday last to visit her friends at Brierley Hill, had not returned; he had caused inquiry be made, but could not obtain any tidings of her, and he feared she had met with some accident. Her sister-in-law, to whose house she was going, was introduced to the Board, but she could give no information as to the whereabouts of her relative, as she had not seen her, though she and her husband had diligently sought her out. They had, moreover, consulted the wise man of Brierley Hill (not Dunn, the celebrated Dudley devil,) who had ruled the planets, and said that as the moon and stars were in the decline, she was no doubt in the earth. He thought he could see she had fallen into the canal and been squeezed into the earth by a boat, and would never more be seen by eye of man; and this the narrator seemed verily to believe, not because of the probability that an aged female might fall into a canal, where there are plenty of them, but because the soothsayer assured her such was the fate of her friend, as indicated by the stars.
```

```{admonition} Death of the "Dudley Fortune Teller", October 1851
:class: dropdown
In *Worcestershire Chronicle*, [Wednesday 15 October 1851](https://britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000350/18511015/024/0005).

DUDLEY. Death of the "Dudley Fortune Teller." — For many years has resided at Netherton, near this place, Theophilus Dunn, strange man, whose real name but few knew, although neither old nor young for miles round were ignorant of the "Dudley Devil." A powerful man he was, of commanding, and of late years, venerable appearance, and somewhat eccentric in manner. He was an oracle as sacred in the belief of thousands in that locality as that of Delphi was two thousand years ago to the people of Greece. He was the chief resource times of difficulty or trial to half the lower classes of society in the district in which he resided. Young men and silly girls, anxious to glance into futurity, went there. Husbands were sought for—wives there found. Disease had its solace in his predicted cure. He was the restorer of lost goods, the discoverer of stolen property, the healer of broken hearts. Some of his predictions were fulfilled, and his reputation spread. His mode of sorcery no one could tell; but there are hundreds who could almost swear that what he foretold always came true. Time crept upon him unawares, however, and he began to totter down the hill of life. He had enjoyed the happiness of the married state, but of late years his wife had lived apart from him, and he resided alone in cottage row of houses of which, we have been informed, he was the owner. On Thursday morning last he did not appear, It was conjectured that something unusual had occurred, and inquiry discovered that the unfortunate old man—he was between sixty and seventy years of age—had put a period to his existence. He had committed the rash act with singular determination, having affixed handkerchief to a beam, then round his neck, and thus strangled himself. He was found, however, upon the floor. Being man of considerable size, the strain of his weight had ultimately broken the handkerchief, but not until he had ceased to live. His death, in a district where his peculiar claims to notice were well known, has excited considerable interest. He was a man who possessed some property, chiefly, we believe, houses. An inquest was held on the body on Saturday last, at the Fox and Goose Inn, Bumble Hole, before W. Robinson, Esq., and jury, when evidence having been adduced showing that the deceased had been labouring under a deranged state of mind for several weeks previously to his death, verdict of "Insanity" was recorded. The deceased was known far and wide for his practices in the "black arts," and many apparently respectable persons, chiefly of the fair sex, have been known to come distances of forty or fifty miles to consult him as to their destiny. Some time ago the deceased lost two turkeys, and not being able, by all the force of his own divination, to trace their whereabouts, he at length questioned half-witted boy, who resided in the neighbourhood, at the same time offering him a shilling if he could give any information concerning the missing turkeys. The boy at once consented, and being cunning enough to secure the money first, he informed the would-be fortune teller, much to his chagrin, that he would find the turkeys down a certain pit-shaft (pointing to it,) adding, with much significance, "I throwed them there myself." We have been informed, however, that this evil genius exercised a beneficial influence upon some delinquents the locality where he resided, and that instances are not wanting where thieves, who had stolen articles, on being informed that "Dunn" said so, restored the property to its rightful owners. It would appear that the deceased, besides considerable property in buildings, has left money deposited in bank. It is stated that he has left a will.
```

```{admonition} An instance of gross superstition, June 1852
:class: dropdown
In *Worcestershire Chronicle*, [Wednesday 30 June 1852](https://britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000350/18520630/037/0006).

The "Dudley Devil" again.—The following instance of gross superstition occurred last week at Bridgnorth. Elizabeth Rutter, Jane Rogers, Jane Perry, and Ann Ravenscroft, were brought before the magistrates to answer charge of Ann Butler, for assaulting her on the 10th inst. It appeared from the evidence that the defendant Rutter had a horse which died suddenly some time ago, and then a cow was taken bad and not able to stand. The complainant was accused of having witched these animals with a spell, obtained from the "Dudley Devil," in consequence of some previous disagreement between the parties. The complainant had admitted to several persons that she carried a spell sewn up in her stays, and there is not much doubt but that when the horse died and the cow was ill, the complainant thought it would be a good chance to impose upon these ignorant people, and to say that she had witched them, by way of retaliation for the wrongs previously done to her. However, on the 10th instant, the complainant, who buys old brass, was requested by Mrs. Rutter to call upon her, and while engaged in looking at some old metal, Mrs. Rutter locked one door, and the servant girl locked the other; Mrs. Rutter then knocked her down with her fist, got her head between her knees, and began tearing her dress open to get at the spell in her stays. As soon as Mrs. Rutter had knocked her down, the three other defendants arrived at the scene of action from the places in which they had been hiding. The result was that, after a good deal of scuffling, the spell was obtained, though in their avidity to get it they tore away a piece of the stays in which it was sewn up. The complainant, when on the floor, told them that it was only a charm for the tooth-ache, which some gipsy had given her, and that if they would let her get up she would give it them, and this she said before the magistrates. Mrs. Rutter admitted knocking her down, and tearing the spell away; and Jane Rogers admitted taking the spell by force out of complainant's hand; but Jane Perry and Ann Ravenscroft denied touching her, though complainant said they held her arms The magistrates fined the two former 6d., and costs 10s. 6d. each, and discharged the latter.
```

```{admonition} A tale from the past? May 1857
:class: dropdown
In *Shrewsbury Chronicle*, [Friday 15 May 1857](https://britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000401/18570515/060/0006).

MORE WITCHCRAFT.—A great deal has been said and written lately about a belief in witchcraft, as though the discovery that such a belief existed amongst the English lower orders in the 19th century was a very wonderful discovery indeed. After all, there is nothing scarcely more prevalent in agricultural districts than a belief in at least something amounting to witchcraft. Everybody nearly in those districts know of the existence of this superstition; but like most common things little has been said about it, because every one was presumed to be as wise as his neighbour on the subject. The following is one case out of many similar ones which are of every day occurrence. A man in this neighbourhood lost a pair of boots. He spoke publicly of the theft, and and even acquainted the police of it; but the boots were not found. At last he determined to have recourse to a noted necromancer, styled rather more truly than euphoniously, the "Dudley devil." A visit was, therefore, paid to the pandemonium of the Satanic gentleman; but what passed at the infernal council has not transpired to the public. The result, however, was most satisfactory, at least to the individual who lost the boots, though hardly so, one might fancy, to thé thief himself. Two months nearly had elapsed since the boots were stolen. One night the man who had lost them went to his door, and there he found a pair of boots. Did his eyes deceive him? Were they —could they be his boots—the stolen boots? He examined them. They were good boots—boots which had evidently been new heeled and tapped; those he lost were sadly out at the sole and welt. But the upper leathers? Yes; they were unmistakeable—they really were the, and nothing but the, lost boots! The Dudley devil had supported his reputation; and doubtless the boots were conveyed to the door on a flash of lightning, or something of that kind. The poor man declares they do in reality smell somewhat of sulphur. The plain probability is, that the thief was equally as superstitious as the loser of the boots, and hearing that the Dudley devil had been consulted, became frightened, and was induced by fear to do that which honesty could not impel to do.
```

```{admonition} His reputation lives on, December 1856
"The Dudley Devil."—The belief in prophetic lore seems to be gaining ground in this locality. The other day a woman was accused of stealing some fowls; she protested her innocence with much indignation, and told her accuser that if he would take her to a man called "The Dudley Devil," he would not only clear her character, but tell who stole the chickens, and where (if living) they could be found!
```

Reputations persist, and even several years after his death, the label of "Dudley Devil" bit be used as a shorthand when referring to tales that involved other cunning folk.
