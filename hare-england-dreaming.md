## A Telling

Not far from where I live, just down the road a little way, there's what I'd call a ginnel, or a snicket, leading down the fenced off side of a house on one side, and a hedgerow on the other...

There are gaps in the hedgerow, looking through into a farmer's field. And if you look carefully, you can see, still, to this day, not rabbits, but hares. 

Once, a man was walking down just such a path, maybe even that path, he'd stopped at such a gap and seen such a hare, sat there, on its hind quarters, watching him. And it got him thinking...

What if he could stun the hare, wring it's neck, take it back to Shanklin market, and get a couple of pennies for it; one for the bar, and one for a chicken, perhaps...

The chicken would lay, eggs, he could sell the eggs, a few more pennies and a few more hens...

Maybe a cockerel, then he could make his own chickens...

Maybe a piglet, fatten it up, sell it on to get more piglets...

Maybe one piglet would be a sow, cover her with a boar, make his own piglets...

Doing quite well now... More pigs, a bit of land to keep them on, what with doing so well, and he'd be a man of means. A reputable farmer himself.

Up on the hill lived old man Beach, a farmer, the farmhouse is still there to this day. His daughter, not much of a looker, but it's a big farm, dairy and arable. No pigs though. They could marry, and then, when old man Beach had taken his last breath, the's have two farms...

And then they'd have a daughter, and she'd be so pretty, not necessarily taking after his wife, but and she'd grow up to marry a prince, or, maybe that's too much, a Lord, a lord of the manor, perhaps. Or, or maybe she'd meet a soldier, or a sailor, or god forbid a musician or a storyteller, or... 

No. He'd have a son. Two sons. ANd he'd be a good Dad, a great Dad, and they;d play football and games, and they;d be interested in the farm. And they'd help out, on the farm, doing this and that, and then more and more. Help feed the animals, muck them out, help with the milking. He could have a lie in for once, what with them doing the milking.

Only, they wouldn't, would they. Idle sods, teenage boys. It;d be their turn to do the milking, 5am a cold frosty November morning, it'd be their turn to do the milking and they'd be in bed, wouldn't they, idle sods that they were snoring and sleeping when they should be out working.

So what he'd do, he creep quietly, quietly, up the stairs, up the stairs to their room, where they'd be snoring, wouldn't they, snoring away and he quietly, quietly, ever so gently open their door, and there they's be snoring away, and he shout:

Oi..... and with that, the hare would take start, and run away across the field, taking all Jack's dreams with it; and Jack would drop the stone, back where he'd find it, and carry on along his way.
